Break the Chain of Anger



Too much time with family may also mean plenty of opportunity for misunderstandings, fights, anger and blow ups. "Can you keep your voice down?" shouted Lily, to her husband with irritation on her face. The daughter and nephew sitting there burst out laughing. And the husband was angry and responded, "I just moved out of the room you were seated in to take this call so that I did not disturb you, and now that you moved here, you are asking me to be quieter!" And then the husband walked away to the corridor... Sounds familiar?


But here is what happened before this conversation:

Lily was trying to set up Zoom to teach her first online class. She was stressed about the technology and this was a relatively new teaching assignment. So Lily postponed all the other important work she had for the online class. But at the moment, the students were making unreasonable requests to postpone the online class. This made Lily very irritated.
We show the anger we have on one person on another, who has no role in the reason for your anger. But the anger passes on like a chain.

What can we do to break this chain?

1. When you know you're angry, withdraw yourself and take time for yourself.

2. Later when you think you're ok, react to the situation.

By creating a distance between the emotional trigger and the reaction, the anger gets dissipated.

Do remember that each of us are going through a lot that we are mostly unaware of. So, compassion and patience will help in this situation.

When in doubt, practice this:

Take a wise break when triggered and create space before you react.

Apologize or make necessary response as quickly as you can.

Don't let your anger get the better of you.
