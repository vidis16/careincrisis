Matters of the Mind

 

Cooking: A Recipe for
Mental Well-being

Cooking is a therapeutic activity for your mind and senses. People derive sensory pleasure through cooking, as it involves the use of multiple senses in the process of cooking. Like it is pleasurable to dive into a tub of ice cream, for some, seeing the phulkas puff up is gratifying. (not that a tub of ice cream is not!) An author says “preparing, cooking, and serving food is…an ‘unexpressed intimacy.’”

Cooking is an elaborate process. Starting from finding the perfect recipe for you (based on the ingredients you have or your likes!) to having the first bite, one experiences a range of emotions. You may feel irritated or angry when the dish does not turn out the way you wanted it to be; you may feel elated when it turns out perfect! You may feel nostalgic with the aroma of some food that you cook. Research shows that cooking is a way to connect with family history or one’s past, by digging into old family recipes. Some of the process involved in cooking such as kneading dough can be a way of releasing pent up emotions. Not only will you have that perfectly smooth dough, but also may also feel lighter in your head and heart.

The process of cooking involves use of gross and fine motor skills. It is an activity that engages your body and your mind; it requires one to be attentive and be in the moment, otherwise, you might have your hand or your dish burnt! 

Research suggests that cooking for others is a way to boost self esteem and confidence. It is also a way to feel happy and connected with others. It creates an affirming bond with the others. As food is a basic need, creating and strengthening a bond through cooking together or cooking for the other may be effective.

As you spend time at home, why don’t you enter the kitchen and prepare a dish for yourself, or for your family? It can be tea/coffee or an elaborate meal. You could help someone else when they are cooking. It can be a way of engaging young children and also build their gross and fine motor skills. Parent-child relationship can be strengthened in the kitchen through sharing of responsibilities. Couples can together cook a meal and have a date night, for kitchen can be a beautiful space for togetherness.

Cooking is an activity of self-care and also a creative process, which can be helpful for your mental well-being.
