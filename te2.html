Thinking Errors: Part 2

This article talks more about thinking errors that we engage in our everyday life.

Helplessness is the thinking trap that a negative life event will impact every domain of your life and that you have no control over them. It makes you believe that there is nothing you can do about the situation whatsoever. This results in a lack of motivation and withdrawal.
On a cognitive level, helplessness manifests as issues with judgement and problem solving. Uncertainity on what to do, what will will happen etc can plague the mind in this thinking trap. On a physical level, helplessness can feel like a paralyzing fear and can cause extreme fatigue.

Overcoming this thinking trap
1. Use evidence- Look for proof as whether or not your thought is actually true and reliable. Chances are, you will not find proof to comfirm your belief.
Or in the event that you do, next thing you could try is
2. Challenge or reframe
Challenging would be to question the helpless thought. "Am I really helpless in this situation? Am I not in control of anything at all?"
In most cases you will find that there is something you can do, and identifying it will make it easier to do.
Reframing would be to modify your thinking in order to look at the situation in a more helpful way that makes you feel optimistic and more in control.
3. Practice
Try out the new way of thinking in your own time and in a safe space. Plan and make use of these strategies in your everyday life. This will help prepare you and strengthen your resolve. With practise, this kind of thinking will become easier with time

All-or-nothing is a thinking error that is also known as black-and-white thinking. It is thinking in polarised manner where you present events and yourself in "either/or" categories. While categorization of information is supposed to help the brain sift through the input it receives, black-and-white thinking leaves a person thinking in extremes, which may be problematic.
For example, a person may think that "I must succeed, otherwise I'm a failure."
Here, success and failure are presented as two distinct categorizations, which may be strictly defined. This restricts one's vision that there can be shades of grey, i.e. many other possibilities between the two absolutes. Thinking in extremes also results in feeling extreme emotions. One may experience immense sadness and disappointment as a result of these thoughts. Through this binary thinking process, you negatively evaluate yourself as well as others.
You may believe that a person loves you, or if s/he doesn't, it means that they hate you. Maybe the other person does not fall into either of the two categories. Maybe it is that "friend zone". 

What can you do if you are someone who thinks all-or-nothing?
Recognize that there can be many possibilities, which lie between the extremes. Consider your thoughts to be on a thermometer that has a range of readings. Understand that your thoughts also fall on a range and not on two sides.
If you want to follow a diet and impose 100% compliance on yourself, missing one day would not bring it to 0%. Rather it would be a little less than 100%. Maybe 90% or 80%.
This also involves developing a positive outlook. Look at a situation and ask what can be a positive you gain out of it.
Try and avoid terms that are extreme. These include always, never, all the time and the like. Remember that there are exceptions or other possibilities.
Seek support from someone you trust to help you identify and challenge these binary thoughts.

Filtering is a bias in which a person focuses only on the negative events and not on the positive events. In other words, they seem to have a filter and this helps them focus on all the negative aspects of a situation. This leads to inaccurate respresentations of situations in a person's mind.

Such filtering is often accompanied by unrealistical high expectations. This involves an expectation on oneself to always doing something well, or do something perfectly. Perfection is the standard against which all effort is judged. Achieving that level of perfection is seen as normal, however not achieving it, is seen as a complete failure. This failure negates all the other times one has succeeded, and rather the focus is solely on the times one has failed.

Filtering can result in depressed mood, poor self-esteem, and unhealthy pessimism. Filtering is one of the primary cognitive distortions seen with people who have depression.

How does one overcome filtering?
By being consciously aware of this, a person can see how prevalent it is, how it affects them etc.
By becoming aware, one can start to examine evidence of different situations to see whether there is anything that can prove such thinking.
Questioning the filtering or countering it with the opposite of the belief will help create doubt in one's mind as to how one sees the situation. This in turn provides a realistic image of the situation itself.
With time and practise, this manner of thinking can be overcome.
